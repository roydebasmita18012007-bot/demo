AM Theory Guide
What is Amplitude Modulation?

In Amplitude Modulation (AM), the amplitude of a high-frequency carrier wave is varied proportionally to the instantaneous value of a lower-frequency message signal. The frequency and phase of the carrier remain constant.

The carrier acts as a "vessel" — it carries no information itself, but its envelope encodes the message.

s(t) = [Ac + m(t)] cos(2πfct)
Modulation Index & Distortion

The modulation index m = Am/Ac controls depth of modulation:

  • m < 1 — Under-modulated: envelope detector works perfectly, no phase reversal.
  • m = 1 — Critical: maximum modulation depth without distortion.
  • m > 1 — Over-modulated: envelope clipping occurs, causing severe harmonic distortion and adjacent-channel interference.
Power & Spectral Efficiency

In standard AM (DSB-TC), most transmitted power is wasted in the carrier:

η = m² / (2 + m²) × 100%

At m=1, only 33.3% of total power carries message information. Techniques:

  • DSB-SC: Carrier suppressed → 100% efficiency, same BW as AM.
  • SSB: One sideband removed → 100% efficient AND half bandwidth.
Sideband Frequencies & Bandwidth

AM modulation creates two spectral replicas around the carrier:

  • LSB (Lower Sideband): fc − fm
  • USB (Upper Sideband): fc + fm

Standard AM/DSB-SC: BW = 2fm
SSB: BW = fm

This is why commercial AM radio stations are spaced 10 kHz apart — each can carry audio up to 5 kHz.

Envelope Detection (RC Demodulator)

A practical envelope detector uses a diode + RC filter. The diode rectifies the AM signal (passes positive half-cycles only). The capacitor charges rapidly to peaks and discharges slowly through R.

RC time constant must satisfy: 1/fc ≪ RC ≪ 1/fm

  • Too small RC → ripple at carrier frequency remains.
  • Too large RC → capacitor can't follow envelope → distortion.

Phasor Diagram Interpretation

In the rotating phasor frame (synchronized to fc), the AM signal decomposes into:

  • ■ Yellow: Carrier phasor Ac (stationary)
  • ■ Green: USB phasor rotating at +fm
  • ■ Cyan: LSB phasor rotating at −fm
  • ■ Magenta: Resultant — the instantaneous envelope

For SSB, only one sideband rotates, creating circular motion rather than linear oscillation.
